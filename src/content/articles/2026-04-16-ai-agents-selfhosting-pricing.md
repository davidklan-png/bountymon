Big Tech just confirmed what we've been watching for months: **Microsoft is building its own OpenClaw-like agent** to compete in the AI assistant gold rush. And while they pitch this as "innovation," the real story is how this agent war is about to hammer your software budget harder than ever.

Microsoft's new Copilot features, inspired by the open source OpenClaw agent, promise enterprise customers "better security controls" than the "famously risky" original. Translation: they learned from OpenClaw's security drama and are now monetizing the fix.

But here's the kicker: **every AI agent comes with a hidden tax**. While Microsoft charges premium seat-based pricing for its Copilot agents, the real cost isn't just the subscription fee. It's the lock-in, the data dependency, and the subscription creep that follows. 

Meanwhile, the data center costs are exploding. This week, the U.S. Energy Information Agency announced they'll force data centers to disclose their power bills. Why? Because AI infrastructure is burning through energy at rates that make the old SaaS pricing models look like a bargain.

Fluidstack, the AI data center startup, just reportedly secured talks for a $1B round at an $18B valuation—months after hitting $7.5B. This isn't sustainable. It's the same playbook we saw with cloud services: predatory pricing followed by inevitable consolidation.

But here's where it gets interesting for software buyers. **The agent revolution is making the self-hosting argument stronger than ever**. When you see Microsoft charging enterprise premiums for local AI agents, and Anthropic burning through billions in data center costs, the math starts to change.

Open-source alternatives like OpenClaw showed us agents could run locally. Now they're proving that with the right setup, you can have agent-driven productivity without Big Tech's subscription treadmill.

The question isn't whether to adopt AI agents—it's who controls them. Microsoft wants to sell you another seat-based license. We think you should run your own.
