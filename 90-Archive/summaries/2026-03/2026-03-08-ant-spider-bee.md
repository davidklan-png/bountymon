# The Ant, the Spider, and the Bee

---

The latest Dwarkesh Podcast seemed like an odd one at first — a deep dive into Renaissance history, not the usual tech and AI conversation. But as historian Ada Palmer traced how knowledge actually moves through generations, I found myself asking a more personal question: How do I engage with the people around me? Do I hoard what I know, do I dazzle people with clever frameworks, or do I actually work with others to make something better than what any of us could build alone?

It turns out Francis Bacon had a metaphor for this. In 1620.

He divided thinkers into three animals:

**The Ant** 🐜

Collects and uses. Raw empiricism. Data without insight. Information hoarding.

**The Spider** 🕷️

Spins webs from its own substance. Pure rationalism. Theory without grounding. Beautiful systems disconnected from reality.

**The Bee** 🐝

The middle path. Gathers from the flowers, but transforms what it gathers. Digests. Synthesizes. Creates something new.

Bacon's point: The ant never innovates. The spider never validates. Only the bee produces honey.

---

## In the AI Era, We're Drowning in Ants

- Prompt engineers scraping the web
- RAG systems retrieving endlessly
- "AI researchers" who are really just data collectors

And we have no shortage of spiders either:

- Theorists spinning elegant frameworks
- Visionaries with zero empirical grounding
- Hot takes masquerading as insight

The bee is rarer. The bee asks:

- What did I gather?
- What does it mean?
- What can I build from this that didn't exist before?

---

## The Chain Behind Any Breakthrough

What Palmer made vivid for me was the chain behind any breakthrough:

Petrarch (1340s) → "Let's recover Roman classics"
↓
Build libraries → Print books → Create literacy
↓
Machiavelli (1500s) → "Use history as a casebook"
↓
Bacon (1600s) → "Use nature as a casebook"

Bacon didn't invent the scientific method from nothing. He became a bee because generations of ants had already done the gathering. The honey only becomes possible after the long, unglamorous work of collecting.

---

## The Personal Hit

That hit me personally. I've spent time being each of these animals — sometimes hoarding knowledge as a kind of job security, sometimes spinning theories that impressed no one (including myself), and occasionally, on a good day, actually helping synthesize something that made the work a little sweeter for everyone involved.

The goal isn't to skip the ant phase. Read broadly. Gather data. Collect examples. You can't transform what you haven't gathered.

But don't stay an ant.

The transformation is where value lives — for you, and for the people around you. This is what separates researchers from aggregators, founders from trend-chasers, collaborators from gatekeepers.

**Gather like an ant. Spin like a spider. But aim to produce like a bee.**
