# The Market for Software Is Being Renegotiated—By Agents  
*Updated research-style article for LinkedIn. Date: 2026-03-06 (Asia/Tokyo).*

## Abstract
Agentic coding isn’t “better autocomplete.” It’s a structural change in how software work gets done: engineers increasingly orchestrate autonomous agents that write, test, and ship changes across repositories—shifting humans toward architecture, risk, and judgment.

This shift is economic, not just technical. As building and adapting software gets cheaper and faster, the value of buying SaaS changes. The near-term market impact is most visible in renewals and contracting models, not on list-price pages.

**Thesis:** Agentic workflows will compress seat-based SaaS expansion, intensify enterprise contract negotiation, shift contracts from “software access” toward “service outcomes,” and accelerate replacement of UI-centric point tools via open source + agents.

---

## 1) Seat-based SaaS is entering its compression era
Seat-based SaaS pricing historically assumed outcomes scale with the number of human users. Agentic workflows weaken that link: one person supervising agents can deliver the output of many, so seat growth can decouple from business growth.

This doesn’t imply immediate list-price collapse. It implies:
- slower expansions,
- seat rationalization at renewal,
- and increased pricing-model experimentation (bundles, credits, usage/outcome hybrids).

Directional market signals are consistent with this shift. Zylo’s SaaS Management Index reports rapid acceleration in spend on AI-native applications (including a reported +108% change in AI-native app spend in its report), reflecting reallocation of budgets rather than simple “more seats everywhere.”

---

## 2) Renewals become strategic negotiations (where impact shows up first)
In enterprise software, the most measurable impacts emerge inside contracts. The credible threat that a buyer can “build or replace” changes negotiation leverage—making renewals strategic events rather than routine purchases.

Recent industry reporting describes increasing pressure on SaaS renewals and highlights the “build alternative” threat as one source of downward pressure.

**Expected renewal patterns (2026–2027):**
1. **Seat rationalization + AI carve-outs**: keep the platform, shrink footprint, reprice AI.
2. **Hybrid pricing**: humans priced one way; agents priced by usage/outcomes.
3. **Bundled agent licenses** (“all you can eat”) used by vendors to lock accounts.

---

## 3) Contracts shift from “software access” to “service outcomes”
As agents take autonomous actions inside business workflows—approving, reconciling, refunding, routing—traditional SaaS terms become a poor fit. Expect contracts to resemble services-style agreements, emphasizing governance, auditability, outcome SLAs, and liability allocation.

Legal analysis is converging on this model. Mayer Brown describes agentic AI agreements evolving from classic SaaS toward services-style structures (including outcome expectations and risk allocation).

**Prediction:** The winners won’t just “add agents.” They’ll sell *auditable execution*—and will be willing to contract for accountability.

---

## 4) “Moats” migrate from UI to data + workflow control
Agents don’t care about dashboards. They care about APIs, permissions, data access, integration reliability, and audit trails. That shifts defensibility away from UI and toward:
- data and proprietary labels,
- identity and governance layers,
- integration surface area (APIs + connectors),
- reliability and compliance posture.

A useful way to think about vulnerability:
- UI-centric point tools are most exposed,
- systems of record are more defensible,
- and categories like CRM/ITSM are a contested “gray zone.”

---

## 5) Open source + agents accelerates replacement by unbundling (the Bountymon thesis)
The most realistic replacement pattern isn’t “rip out everything.” It’s unbundling:
1) choose a vulnerable function (thin UI, repetitive workflow),
2) replace the core with open-source primitives,
3) add agents to deliver automation + usability,
4) integrate via APIs instead of migrating the whole stack.

This is where **Bountymon** fits: a “takedown engine” that spotlights open-source replacements and incentivizes targeted displacement of oversized SaaS products—especially where the incumbent moat is UI + habit.

---

## 6) What to watch in 2026–2028 (predictions)
### Prediction A — Expansion slows before prices fall
Seat growth will soften (or reverse) in functions where agents reduce human throughput requirements. The macro signal will appear in renewal behavior and realized pricing, not list prices.

### Prediction B — Procurement becomes “agent-aware”
Expect new standards for:
- auditability (what did the agent do? why?),
- cost controls (caps, meters, true-ups),
- and contractual accountability (outcome SLAs).

### Prediction C — Moats become orchestration and governance
Defensibility concentrates in platforms that control identity, data access, workflow execution, and compliance—not just “best UI.”

### Prediction D — OSS displacement expands from dev tools to business workflows
Open source, once combined with agentic automation, will compete more effectively in non-developer domains (support operations, internal tooling, repetitive approvals), accelerating replacement of UI-centric point solutions.

---

## Practical implications
### For buyers (CIO / Procurement / Ops)
- Use “OSS + agents” as renewal leverage even if you don’t execute replacement.
- Demand governance and auditability as first-class requirements.
- Measure value in throughput/cycle time, not feature checklists.

### For builders (founders / product / GTM)
- Plan for pricing-model drift away from pure seats (hybrids/outcomes).
- Build moats in data/control/integration/governance.
- Target replacements where the incumbent moat is thin: repetitive workflows + manageable integrations.
