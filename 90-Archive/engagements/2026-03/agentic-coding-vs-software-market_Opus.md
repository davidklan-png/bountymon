# The Code That Writes Itself Is Rewriting the Market

**How Agentic Coding Is Forcing the Biggest Reckoning in Enterprise Software Since the Cloud**

---

Between January 15 and February 14, 2026, something unprecedented happened. Approximately $2 trillion in market capitalization evaporated from the software sector [1]. Not because of a recession. Not because of regulation. Because AI agents started doing the jobs that enterprise software was sold to support.

The press called it the "SaaSpocalypse" [2]. Whether that term sticks or fades, the structural shift underneath it is real, measurable, and accelerating. And for anyone making technology decisions — buying software, negotiating contracts, or planning infrastructure — the implications demand attention right now.

This article is my attempt to cut through the noise and draw conclusions from what the data actually shows.

---

## The Shift: From Writing Code to Orchestrating Agents

Let's ground this in what's actually changed. In 2025, engineering teams discovered that AI could handle entire implementation workflows — writing tests, debugging failures, navigating complex codebases [3]. By early 2026, roughly 85% of developers were regularly using AI coding tools [4], and Gartner forecasts that 60% of new code will be AI-generated by year's end. At Google and Microsoft, that figure already sits around 30% [5].

But the real story isn't autocomplete getting better. It's that the role of the engineer is fundamentally changing. Developers are shifting from writing code line-by-line to coordinating autonomous agents that write code, focusing their own expertise on architecture, system design, and strategic judgment [3]. Anthropic's 2026 Agentic Coding Trends Report documents this in detail: developers now use AI in approximately 60% of their work, though they fully delegate only 0–20% of tasks [6]. The human remains in the loop — but the loop has gotten dramatically wider.

The scale of organizational impact is already measurable. TELUS teams created over 13,000 custom AI solutions while shipping engineering code 30% faster, saving over 500,000 hours total. Zapier achieved 97% AI adoption across their entire organization with 800+ agents deployed internally [3]. These aren't pilot programs — they're operational at scale.

This is the foundation that makes everything else in this article possible. When it becomes cheap and fast to build software, the value of buying pre-built software changes.

---

## The $285 Billion Question: Is SaaS Pricing Structurally Broken?

Here's where it gets consequential for anyone managing technology budgets.

The per-seat pricing model that powered two decades of SaaS growth is cracking. The logic is straightforward: if one person equipped with AI agents can accomplish the work of five, you don't need five seats [7]. Multiple SaaS companies reported slowing growth in Q4 2025 earnings — not because AI failed to boost productivity, but precisely because it succeeded. Customers are reducing software seats, not adding them [7].

The numbers are striking. According to Zylo's 2026 SaaS Management Index, annual SaaS spend rose 8% across organizations while the number of applications held essentially flat at around 305 [8]. Costs are climbing not because companies are buying more tools, but because vendors are layering in AI tiers, shifting to consumption pricing, and charging premiums that inflate spend without adding new capabilities [8]. Meanwhile, spending on AI-native applications — where AI is the core product — jumped 108% year over year [8].

The global SaaS market is on track to grow from $266 billion in 2024 to roughly $315 billion by early 2026 [9], but beneath that headline growth, the economics of every contract are being rewritten. Gartner forecasts enterprise software spend rising at least 40% by 2027, with generative AI as the primary accelerant [9]. Global spending on AI-enabled applications could reach $644 billion in 2025 alone, a 76.4% increase from 2024 [9].

In other words: the money is moving. It's leaving traditional per-seat software and flowing toward AI-native alternatives and internal build capabilities. The question for every procurement leader is whether their current contracts reflect this new reality or the old one.

---

## Enterprise Contracts: The Leverage Has Shifted

This is the section I'd pay closest attention to if I were sitting across from a vendor at renewal time.

SaaS vendors know their customers are evaluating AI alternatives. As TechCrunch reported, customers now have the ultimate contract negotiation tool in their pockets. If they don't like a vendor's prices, they can more easily than ever build their own alternative. Even if they never actually build it, the credible threat creates downward pressure on renewal contracts [2].

We're already seeing this play out in new contract structures. Constellation Research identifies what they call "Agentic Enterprise License Agreements" (ALEAs) — essentially all-you-can-eat pricing for agentic AI capabilities that vendors are offering to lock in customers [10]. Some vendors are reportedly willing to sign these at a loss, betting on the lifetime value of the relationship [10]. That's a telling signal about where the power sits right now.

The contract model itself is evolving. Legal firm Mayer Brown published analysis in February 2026 arguing that agentic AI contracts increasingly resemble managed services or outsourcing agreements rather than traditional SaaS terms [11]. When an AI agent is approving refunds, reconciling invoices, or triggering payments inside live workflows, the liability and governance frameworks of a standard SaaS agreement simply don't fit. Enterprises are pushing for expanded indemnities, audit rights, and clearer risk allocation [11].

The pricing model evolution is equally dramatic. Seat-based and flat-fee models are losing ground to usage-based and hybrid structures that tie price to activity like data processed, tokens consumed, or API calls made [12]. Gartner projects that by 2030, at least 40% of enterprise SaaS spend will shift toward usage-, agent-, or outcome-based pricing [13].

For project managers and IT leaders, the practical takeaway is this: your next renewal negotiation is not a routine event. It's a strategic opportunity to restructure terms around the new economics.

---

## The "Moat" Question: Which Applications Are Actually Defensible?

This is where I think the conversation gets most interesting — and where a lot of the hot takes get it wrong.

The narrative that AI agents will replace all enterprise software is overstated. Gartner predicts that 35% of point-product SaaS tools will be replaced by AI agents by 2030 — which means 65% will survive, albeit likely in evolved forms [7]. Deloitte's position is even more measured: they predict full SaaS replacement by agents won't happen in 2026 and will likely take at least five years, even with rapid investment [13].

The AI agents market itself provides important context. Currently valued at $7.84 billion in 2025, it's projected to reach $52.62 billion by 2030 at a 46.3% CAGR [14]. That's significant growth — but it's additive to the software market, not a wholesale replacement of it.

The pattern that's actually emerging is selective unbundling. Commoditized point solutions — tools built around repetitive data entry, status updates, report generation, or template-based communication — are the most vulnerable [1]. These are the categories where AI agents can replicate the core functionality without the overhead of a separate platform.

But platforms with deep data moats, strong network effects, and regulatory compliance infrastructure are in a different position entirely. Klarna ditching Salesforce's CRM in favor of a homegrown AI system made headlines in late 2024 [2] — but Klarna is a technology company with engineering depth. Most enterprises don't have that luxury, and the complexity of migrating off deeply integrated platforms like SAP, ServiceNow, or Workday shouldn't be underestimated.

Notably, Booz Allen CTO Bill Vass argues that many SaaS vendors will successfully pivot to selling AI agents themselves, and internal agentic systems haven't yet matured to the point where companies are broadly cutting SaaS contracts [15]. The reality on the ground is messier than the headlines suggest.

My read on where the defensible moats actually sit:

**Vulnerable:** Standalone analytics dashboards, basic workflow automation, simple project tracking, template-driven communication tools, and single-function utilities that wrap a thin UI around data.

**Defensible (for now):** Platforms that serve as systems of record for regulated data, deeply integrated ERP and HRIS systems, tools with strong multi-sided network effects, and platforms where the switching cost includes not just technology migration but business process reengineering.

**The grey zone:** CRM, ITSM, and mid-market SaaS that have meaningful integrations but whose core functions are increasingly reproducible by agents. This is where the most interesting battles will play out over the next two years.

---

## My Conclusion: The Impact Is Real, But Uneven

So, is there quantifiable impact from this AI revolution on the software market? Absolutely. A $2 trillion market correction [1], a 108% surge in AI-native spending [8], and the emergence of entirely new contract structures [10][11] aren't noise. They're signal.

But the impact is deeply uneven, and the people who will navigate it best are those who resist both extremes — neither dismissing it as hype nor treating it as an overnight replacement of everything.

Here's where I land after studying the data:

**The build-vs-buy equation has permanently shifted.** The cost of creating a new application has plummeted. This doesn't mean everyone should build everything — maintenance costs may actually be rising for AI-generated code [16], and organizational capability matters enormously. But the credible option to build changes every vendor negotiation [2].

**Per-seat pricing is entering its twilight.** Not dying overnight, but structurally weakening. Hybrid models blending fixed fees with usage-based and outcome-based components are gaining traction rapidly [12][13]. If you're locking into long-term per-seat contracts today without renegotiation clauses, you're likely overpaying.

**The real strategic question isn't "AI or software" — it's "where does human judgment still matter?"** The organizations pulling ahead aren't eliminating engineers or replacing all their tools. They're making expert judgment count where it matters most: architecture, security, system design, and decisions that carry real risk [3][6]. Everything else is becoming delegatable.

**Infrastructure leaders should be preparing now.** Not by ripping and replacing their software stack, but by auditing every SaaS tool against a simple question: could an AI agent replicate 80% of what we use this for? If the answer is yes, that's your negotiation leverage at renewal — whether you actually build the alternative or not [1][2].

The software market isn't dying. But it's being repriced, rebundled, and restructured around a new assumption: that intelligence is cheap, and the scarce resource is judgment. The organizations that internalize this fastest will define what the enterprise stack looks like in 2030.
